The system

Five layers, one direction of flow

Data moves top to bottom. Each connector names the contract passed between layers — the seam you can test in isolation. The dashed adapter is what lets you bolt on a second device (a Huawei Watch D2 for blood pressure) later without rewriting anything above it.

Data flow

One sample's journey, end to end

The order is the contract — each step hands a well-defined payload to the next, so you can build and test them one at a time.

1

Connect

You authorize your Fitbit account once via Google OAuth. The backend stores an encrypted refresh_token.

2

Ingest

A webhook (or scheduled pull) fetches new samples. The adapter normalizes them into one canonical shape.

3

Store

Samples land in raw_samples. Nothing is analyzed yet — this layer is just the durable record.

4

Summarize

A nightly job computes rollups, baselines, and anomaly flags into daily_summaries.

5

Interpret

A weekly job sends the summaries — not raw data — to Claude and caches the narrative into insights.

6

Render

The dashboard reads cached summaries and insights. Page loads never call Claude, so they're fast and free.

Design decisions

The four calls that shape everything

Cost & quality

Aggregate before you ask Claude

Never stream the raw firehose to the model. Pre-compute summaries in your own code, then ask Claude to interpret them. Cheaper tokens, sharper answers, deterministic math stays deterministic.

Extensibility

A normalization adapter per source

Everything above L2 speaks one canonical schema. Adding the Huawei Watch D2 for blood pressure later is a new adapter, not a rewrite. This one boundary is what protects the whole app.

Access model

Personal now, reviewed later

For you + your mother, a personal app gives full, free access including intraday data. Opening it to strangers means Google's app review and stricter rate-limit handling — a gate, not a paywall.

Caching

Insights are persisted, not live

Claude runs on a schedule and writes to the database. The UI reads that cache. This is what keeps a “free for everyone” version from quietly running up an API bill on every page view.

Recommended stack

Solo-friendly, boring on purpose

Every pick optimizes for one person shipping and maintaining this without a team. Swap freely — the layers, not the vendors, are the architecture.

Build order

Ship a thin slice, then widen it

Each milestone is usable on its own. Don't build L4 before L1 works end to end for a single metric.

M1

OAuth + one metric, end to end

Connect your own Fitbit Air, pull heart rate, store it, show a raw chart. Proves the whole pipe.

M2

Summaries + full dashboard

Nightly rollups and baselines for both people; charts for HR, sleep, HRV, SpO₂, steps, temperature.

M3

Claude weekly readout

Scheduled insight generation, cached to the database, surfaced as cards. Your first “gold.”

M4

Near-real-time + alerts

Webhook ingestion and anomaly banners — resting-HR creep, SpO₂ dips, HRV drops worth a look.

M5

Add BP source · open to others

Plug in a Huawei Watch D2 adapter for cuff-grade blood pressure, then take it through Google's review to share.

Cardiac care

Your mother is a post-bypass patient, so treat this app as tracking and decision-support, never diagnosis. Anomaly flags prompt a conversation with her cardiologist — they don't replace one. And when blood pressure enters the picture, use only a validated cuff device (the Watch D2), never an optical PPG estimate, for any number she or her doctor might act on.
